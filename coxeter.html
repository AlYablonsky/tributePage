Here is a timeline of Dr. Coxeter's Life

  • 1907 - Born in London, England
  • 1928, 1931 - Earned a Bachelors and Doctorate in Mathematics at Trinity College in Cambridge England
  • 1932-1934 - Spent a further year at Princeton as a Procter Fellow
  • 1936 - Moved to the University of Toronto where he worked for the next sixty years
  • 1938- Published "The Fifty-Nine Icosahedra" with P. Du Val, H.T. Flather, and John Flinders Petrie
  • 1948-1950 - Became a full Professor and then elected a Fellow of the Royal Society in 1950
  • 1954 - Met Maurits Escher and his work on geometric figures helped inspire some of Escher's works, particularly the Circle Limit series based on hyperbolic tessellations
  • 1954 - Published along with M. S. Longuet-Higgins and J. C. P. Miller the full list of uniform polyhedra
  • 1973 - Published an updated version of Regular Polytopes
  • 1978 the Canadian Mathematical Society has awarded the Coxeter–James Prize in his honor.
  • 1990 - Became a Foreign Member of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences
  • 1997 - Received a Sylvester Medal from the Royal Society and was made a Companion of the Order of Canada
  • 2003 - Died (aged 96) Toronto, Ontario, Canada

What is a Polytope?

In geometry, polytope means the generalization to any dimension of the sequence: polygon in two dimensions, polyhedron in three dimensions, and polychoron in four dimensions. A regular polytope in n dimensions may be defined as having regular facets [(n − 1)-faces] and regular vertex figures. These two conditions are sufficient to ensure that all faces are alike and all vertices are alike.



In 0-D and 1-D

0 and 1 dimension correspond to the point and line segment respectively, nothing more needs to be said.



In 2-D

A regular polygon is a polygon that is equiangular (all angles are equal in measure) and equilateral (all sides have the same length). Regular polygons may be convex or star. A convex polygon is a simple polygon (not self-intersecting) in which no line segment between two points on the boundary ever goes outside the polygon. There are infinitely many regular polygons. Examples of regular convex polygons include the triangle, square, pentagon, hexagon...etc. Examples of regular non-convex polygons (polygrams) include the pentagram.

An n-sided convex regular polygon is denoted by its Schläfli" symbol {n}. For an n-sided star polygon, the Schläfli symbol is modified to indicate the density or "starriness" m of the polygon, as {n/m}. If m is 2, for example, then every second point is joined. If m is 3, then every third point is joined. The boundary of the polygon winds around the center m times.

In 3-D

A regular polyhedron is identified by its Schläfli symbol of the form {n, m}, where n is the number of sides of each face and m the number of faces meeting at each vertex. There are 5 finite convex regular polyhedra, known as the Platonic solids. These are the: tetrahedron {3, 3}, cube {4, 3}, octahedron {3, 4}, dodecahedron {5, 3} and icosahedron {3, 5}. There are also four regular star polyhedra known as Kepler-Poinsot polyhedra, making nine regular polyhedra in all.

And in 4-D?

In mathematics, a regular 4-polytope is a regular four-dimensional polytope. They are the four-dimensional analogs of the regular polyhedra in three dimensions and the regular polygons in two dimensions. Regular 4-polytopes were first described by the Swiss mathematician Ludwig Schläfli in the mid-19th century, although the full set were not discovered until later. There are six convex and ten star regular 4-polytopes, giving a total of sixteen. Note the 24-cell polychoron is unique to four dimensions and has no three dimensional analogue. Needless to say, the four-dimensional shapes are the most interesting shapes with the greatest variety.

And in 5-D and up?

Oddly enough, in ≥ 5 dimensions, life becomes boring again and there are only three regular polytera (5-D polytopes), polypeta (6-D polytopes)..etc. They are the analogues of the tetrahedron, cube and octahedron respectively.

Simplex (n+1) series

A four dimensional 5-cell is made of 5 tetrahedra; a tetrahedron is made of 4 triangles; a triangle is made of 3 lines; a line segment is made of 2 points.

The first series is called the "simplex series". It goes as follows: two points connect to form a line segment, then a third point at the right position in the second dimension connects to both points on the line to form an equilateral triangle, then a fourth point at the right location in the third dimension connects with the other three points of the equilateral triangle to form a tetrahedron, then a fifth point at the right location in the fourth dimension connects to the other four points of the tetrahedron to form a 5-cell...etc.

Tesseract (2n) series

A four dimensional tesseract is made of 8 cubes; a cube is made of 6 squares; a square is made of 4 line segments; a line segment is made of 2 points.

The second series is called the "tesseract" or cubic series. Here two points are connected to form a line segment, then the points of each line segment are connected to the points of another parrallel line segment placed at the right location in a second dimension to form a square, then the points of a different parallel square at just the right location in the third dimension are connected to the points of the original square to form a cube, then the points of another parrallel cube at just the right location in the fourth dimension are connected to the points of the original cube to form a hypercube or tesseract...etc.

Orthoplex (2n) series

A four dimensional 16 cell is made of 16 tetrahedra; an octahedra is made of 8 triangles; a square is made of 4 line segments; a line segment is made of 2 points.

And finally the third series or "orthoplex series". First two points are connected to form a line segment. Then a second line segment is placed perpendicular to the first and the outer points of both line segments are connected to form a 45° tilted square. Then a third line segment is placed perpendicularly to the other two line segments in the third dimension. Connecting all of these points makes an octahedron. Placing another line segment perpendicularly in the fouth dimension to the other three and connecting all of the outer points will make a 16-cell polychoron...etc.
